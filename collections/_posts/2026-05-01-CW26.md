Last week, the CAKE team travelled to Belfast to attend the Collaborations Workshop 2026 (CW26). The Software Sustainability Institute's annual Collaborations Workshop (CW) is an immersive, three-day unconference, which emphasises active collaborations, dynamic discussions, and hands-on problem-solving.

*Eleanor Broadway reflects on a packed few days of workshops, delivering a keynote, and a very successful hack day!*

## Oops, did we do it right? Lessons from successful collaborations

On the first day of the conference, Eva Fernandez Amez and I delivered a workshop focused on mapping the journeys of collaborations: exploring what makes collaborations successful, what resources and support people benefit from along the way, and how we can turn an “Oops!” into an “Aha!”.

The session sparked some really great interesting stories from participants about the realities of collaborative work, the hidden challenges people encounter, and the practical ways we can improve support structures for teams and communities.

We particularly explored:

- Science communication and the skill of gathering information from domain experts within time constraints, when people move away from a collaboration. 
- Making friends! How professional relationships can be challenging, and essential to provide a sense of belonging, community and collective buy-in to an output. 
- Hybrid vs In-person: Can successful collaborations happen purely online? 

We had great discussions with participants throughout the workshop and came away with lots of valuable insights and reflections! 

*Our write-up and outcomes will be published on [CAKEBox](https://www.cake.ac.uk/CAKEbox/) in the coming weeks.*

## The Work Behind the Work: Building Community & Careers 

I was very pleased to be invited to deliver a keynote during the workshop.  

My talk focused on how my career in HPC has been shaped not only by technical interests, but also by participating in volunteering and community-building initiatives such as [Women in HPC](https://womeninhpc.org/) and outreach activities. I discussed how I discovered these communities and how my involvement has grown since, now in a leadership role for both. Through these experiences, I have developed some really essential professional skills that now directly impact my day-to-day work — from communication and leadership to facilitation and project coordination. In particular, I highlighted how I was asked to become a co-I for the CAKE project because of this experience and opportunities! 

Volunteering initatives, networks and groups are often how we communicate across disciplines, institutions and silos on really key issues. For example, EDI, career pathways, climate sustainability, and many more. Community building is essential if our research and technical communities are to remain healthy, inclusive and sustainable. Yet this work is often carried by a small number of people, under-resourced and difficult to sustain long-term.

So, a central theme of my talk was the need to move community work from being viewed as “extra” work to being recognised as essential work — embedded within careers and supported as a shared responsibility across organisations and institutions. We want to keep community voices central to change, while ensuring that participation is supported, visible and rewarding.

I asked people to reflect on these three actions: 

### Learn: Look at the communities you’re part of. Ask: 
* Who is holding the community together? 
* How is that work being supported? 
* How is it recognised? 
* What could you do to improve that?

### Make it visible: 
* Talk about community work in meetings and annual reviews
* Document contributions
* Showcase the value it creates 

### Make it count: Community building work builds essential skills.
* Translate this “extra” work into your capabilities
* Name the skills you are building
* Build your career, whilst giving back

## Hack Day

CW26 concluded with the traditional hack day — one of the highlights of the event. On Wednesday evening, attendees pitched ideas for collaborative hack-day projects. Participants then formed teams around the ideas they were most excited about, before spending Thursday building prototypes and resources. 

I joined the “What kind of community supporter am I?” group, where we worked on creating a short, fun quiz designed to help people identify their “community cook” type. The quiz introduces different styles of community support, including:

- The Influencer Cook
- The Community Cook
- The Hostess with the Mostest
- And more!

The goal is to help people find language for the support work they are already doing, while also highlighting the skills involved and some common pitfalls associated with each role.

Want to find out what kind of community supporter you are? Try the first prototype of the quiz here: [Community Supporter Types Quiz](https://www.rcmcooperative.com/community-supporter-types/?utm_source=chatgpt.com)

And even better — our project actually won the hack day!
